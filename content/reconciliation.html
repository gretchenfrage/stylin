Reconciliation

        "I love you," says the image of my sister, her voice warm and sincere and hurting from distance.

        "I hope you'll visit soon," she adds, with less warmth and more pain and just as much sincerity.

        The statement catches me off guard. I freeze for a moment, staring at her image. I wish it was some hologram, or even a flat display. Anything that wouldn't feel so real, only to vanish as quickly as it appears. But the image is perfect on account of not existing at all, nothing but nerve signals pulsed into my brain by the Interface.

        "Take care," I respond, avoiding a discussion I don't want to have and a promise that I know I couldn't keep.

        I will the call to end. The Interface hears my thought and her image blinks out with a short chiming note.

        I breathe out a defeated sigh. I close my eyes to listen and think. My breath whispers in and out of my nose. My heart beats rhythmically, shockwaves of blood pulsing up my neck and into my ears. I brush my hands against my soft, smooth, synthetic jeans as its smart fibers clean themselves of the sweat which rubbed off my fingers. The Alcubierre-Wendt engine hums in many low and high frequencies, strong and steady like a workhorse as it bends spacetime around my transport.

        Why find loopholes in the laws of our universe if we're just going to use them to get further away from each other?

        No answer appears.

        "Show me home," I speak out loud, my eyes still closed. The dark-pink phantasms behind my eyelids fall away to reveal my family's home by the forest. The humanly imperfect cuts of the criss-crossed eidlerwood logs tell the story of my mother constructing the cabin in the ancient tradition of metal tools. I turn around and feel the warm wind brush over my skin from the forest of massive, lush trees under which fauna, most native but some from Earth, enjoy their simple life.

        I remember when I was a child and I found the cabin to be antiquated and amish and I pitied the simple creatures of the forest. I understand now that those beasts are more happy and innocent than humans will ever be again. I wonder whether I would still be out here if I had realized that sooner.

        I open my eyes, vanishing the forest, the cabin, the warm wind, the smell of grass, the chirping birds, the sun on my face. I stand in my transport in the engine room, electrical channels and mass inverters arching around the room before consolidating in the glowing white box in the middle of the back wall. I walk forward and up the steep, knurled, metal staircase into the cabin. The silver painted-maroon walls arch around me forming a humble-sized living room decorated with paintings and photos and wooden trinkets. I look to the left through the window and into space at thousands of foreign constellations. Their brilliance is beautiful but without a horizon they lose their effect faster than old sci-fi characters would leave you to believe.

        I walk to the right to the pantry, and I will a coffee to form. The reconfigurator starts humming as it produces a glowing white amorphous orb of matter which spends a minute taking the shape and molecular structure of something not entirely unlike a ceramic mug of black coffee. I take a sip, and I feel comforted.

        I walk to the corner of the room where an armchair rests and I sink into its familiar leather. The leather holds itself together strongly but wears scratches and scuffs from years of use from myself and my mother before me. I take note of these scuffs whenever I sit because they say as clear as writing that the leather is real and dead and will not heal nor clean itself. I remember when my mother first bought the chair and I was thirteen and I didn't understand why she would do such a thing.

        "Mom," I asked with a hint of rising inflection.

        "Alice," she responded playfully, moving the chair across our hardwood floor to a place where it would look over the forest.

        "Why did you buy that? It's handmade and crude. It's rough and it'll wear and stain and you'll just have to fix it and clean it and throw it away."

        "It's not supposed to take care of itself. That's what animals do. Dead things don't heal, don't clean themselves."

        "Why not? It's easier... in every way."

        She stopped and paused.

        "Alice, how do you feel about your pants?"

        The question caught me off guard.

        "I don't know. Nothing. They're pants. They're comfortable and they work. They clean and fix themselves. I don't know. I don't care about them."

        "And how do you feel about your bed?" she continued.

        "Uh, it works," I answered. "It's soft and it cleans and fixes itself. It keeps itself exactly as soft and cool as I enjoy it."

        "Mmhm," she said, as if she expected me to understand something. "That's the world we've built for ourselves. The world works. It cleans and fixes itself and we don't have to do any work. It hears our thoughts and makes itself exactly how we want it."

        She paused, then continued.

        "This chair, this cabin, they're more interesting. They won't work for you, you get out what you put in. If you want them clean, you clean them. If you want them pristine, you repair them. And when you rest at the end of the day, you learn to appreciate the way they are, and the work you did to get that. That's how we've lived life for a million years, and that's how we're supposed to do it.

        I rest in the chair, feeling the wooden carvings on the arms with my hands as I mull over that wisdom as if it were a piece of chocolate. I miss her.

        How close am I, I wonder. Twenty minutes away, the Interface lets me know. No words are exchanged.

        I sluggishly stand and walk out of the front of the living room and into the helm. Metal-framed windows arc from the body of the ship into a cockpit in the shape of a toucan's beak. I rest my hands on the back of the white, polymeroid seat and look forward at the red star I am approaching. I continue sipping my coffee.

The transport's humming drops an octave as the engine releases its claws from spacetime and drifts forward at sublight speeds. It descends to the orbiting platform with strength and precision and gentleness and locks into a hover a few inches above the ground. The platform spans for a kilometer in each direction, its metal octagonal face forming a matte reflector of what warm red light passes through the habitat bubble. Under the platform the planet rages as hot as a blast furnace, spewing acid rain and deadly sandstorms and casting bolts of lightning that would put Thor to shame.
